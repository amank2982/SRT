Financial Terms Explained

Our comprehensive glossary covers all the essential stock market terminology, from basic concepts to advanced trading strategies. Use the alphabetical navigation or search function to find the terms you need.

A

Alpha

A measure of an investment's performance on a risk-adjusted basis. Alpha compares the returns of an investment to a benchmark index, taking volatility into account.

Ask Price

The lowest price a seller is willing to accept for a security. Also known as the "offer" price, it's contrasted with the bid price (what buyers are willing to pay).

Asset Allocation

An investment strategy that aims to balance risk and reward by dividing a portfolio among different asset categories such as stocks, bonds, and cash.

B

Bear Market

A market condition where prices are falling or expected to fall, typically defined as a 20% or more decline from recent highs across a broad market index.

Beta

A measure of a stock's volatility in relation to the overall market. A beta of 1 indicates the stock moves with the market, while >1 means more volatile and <1 means less volatile.

Blue Chip

Stocks of large, well-established, and financially sound companies with a history of reliable performance and dividend payments.

C

Capital Gains

The profit from the sale of an asset (like stocks) where the sale price exceeds the purchase price. Capital gains may be short-term (held <1 year) or long-term (held >1 year).

Compound Interest

Interest calculated on the initial principal and also on the accumulated interest of previous periods, allowing investments to grow exponentially over time.

Candlestick Chart

A style of financial chart used to describe price movements of a security, showing the open, high, low, and closing prices for each time period.

D

Dividend

A portion of a company's earnings distributed to shareholders, typically paid quarterly. Expressed as a dollar amount per share or as a percentage (dividend yield).

Diversification

A risk management strategy that mixes a wide variety of investments within a portfolio to reduce exposure to any single asset or risk.

Day Trading

The practice of buying and selling securities within the same trading day, with all positions closed before the market closes to avoid overnight risk.

E

Earnings Per Share (EPS)

A company's net profit divided by the number of common shares it has outstanding, indicating how much money a company makes for each share of its stock.

Exchange-Traded Fund (ETF)

A type of security that tracks an index, sector, commodity, or other assets but trades like a stock on an exchange.

Equity

Ownership interest in a corporation in the form of common stock or preferred stock.

F

Fundamental Analysis

A method of evaluating securities by examining related economic, financial, and other qualitative and quantitative factors.

Futures

Financial contracts obligating the buyer to purchase an asset or the seller to sell an asset at a predetermined future date and price.

Float

The number of a company's shares available for trading by the public.

G

Growth Stock

Shares in a company expected to grow at an above-average rate compared to other companies in the market.

Golden Cross

A chart pattern that occurs when a short-term moving average crosses above a long-term moving average, considered a bullish signal.

Gross Domestic Product (GDP)

The total monetary value of all goods and services produced within a country's borders in a specific time period.

H

Hedge

An investment made to reduce the risk of adverse price movements in an asset.

High-Frequency Trading (HFT)

A type of algorithmic trading characterized by high speeds and high turnover rates.

Holding Period

The length of time an investment is held by an investor, which affects tax treatment of capital gains.

I

Initial Public Offering (IPO)

The first sale of stock by a private company to the public, often used to raise expansion capital.

Index

A statistical measure of change in an economy or securities market, represented as a standardized value.

Inflation

The rate at which the general level of prices for goods and services is rising, eroding purchasing power.

J

Junk Bond

A high-yield, high-risk bond typically issued by companies with questionable credit strength.

January Effect

A seasonal increase in stock prices during the month of January, attributed to year-end tax considerations.

K

Key Performance Indicator (KPI)

A measurable value that demonstrates how effectively a company is achieving key business objectives.

Kangaroo Bond

A foreign bond issued in the Australian market by non-Australian firms and denominated in Australian currency.

L

Liquidity

The degree to which an asset can be quickly bought or sold in the market without affecting its price.

Leverage

The use of borrowed capital to increase the potential return of an investment.

Limit Order

An order to buy or sell a stock at a specific price or better.

M

Market Capitalization

The total market value of a company's outstanding shares, calculated as share price times number of shares.

Moving Average

A technical analysis tool that smooths out price data by creating a constantly updated average price.

Margin

Borrowed money used to purchase securities, with the securities themselves serving as collateral.

N

Net Asset Value (NAV)

The value per share of a mutual fund or ETF, calculated by dividing total assets minus liabilities by number of shares outstanding.

Nasdaq

An electronic global marketplace for buying and selling securities, and the benchmark index for U.S. technology stocks.

O

Open Interest

The total number of outstanding derivative contracts that have not been settled.

Options

Financial derivatives that give buyers the right, but not the obligation, to buy or sell an asset at an agreed-upon price within a certain period.

P

Price-to-Earnings Ratio (P/E)

A valuation ratio comparing a company's current share price to its per-share earnings.

Portfolio

A collection of financial investments like stocks, bonds, commodities, cash, and cash equivalents.

Preferred Stock

A class of ownership with higher claim on assets and earnings than common stock, typically with fixed dividends.

Q

Quantitative Easing (QE)

A monetary policy where central banks purchase securities to increase money supply and encourage lending.

Quarterly Report

A summary of a company's financial performance issued every three months (quarter).

R

Return on Investment (ROI)

A performance measure used to evaluate the efficiency of an investment.

Resistance

A price level at which selling is thought to be strong enough to prevent the price from rising further.

Russell 2000

A small-cap stock market index of the bottom 2,000 stocks in the Russell 3000 Index.

S

Short Selling

The sale of a security that is not owned by the seller, with the intention of buying it back later at a lower price.

S&P 500

A market-capitalization-weighted index of 500 large companies listed on U.S. stock exchanges.

Support

A price level at which buying is thought to be strong enough to prevent the price from declining further.

T

Technical Analysis

A trading discipline evaluating investments by analyzing statistical trends from trading activity.

Treasury Bills (T-Bills)

Short-term U.S. government debt obligations with maturities of one year or less.

Turnover

The percentage of a portfolio's securities replaced in a given year.

U

Underwriter

A financial specialist who works with companies to bring new securities to market.

Unrealized Gain/Loss

A potential profit or loss on an investment that hasn't been sold yet (also called "paper" gain/loss).

V

Volatility

A statistical measure of the dispersion of returns for a given security or market index.

Value Stock

A stock that tends to trade at a lower price relative to its fundamentals and is thus considered undervalued.

W

Wall Street

A metonym for the financial markets and financial institutions of the United States.

Warrant

A derivative that gives the holder the right to buy the underlying stock at a certain price before expiration.

X

Ex-Dividend Date

The date on which a stock begins trading without the value of its next dividend payment.

Y

Yield

The income return on an investment, such as interest or dividends received.

Yield Curve

A line plotting yields (interest rates) of bonds with equal credit quality but differing maturity dates.

Z

Zero-Coupon Bond

A debt security that doesn't pay interest but is traded at a deep discount, rendering profit at maturity.
